# How to Reduce Food Waste at Home — 10 Simple Tips for Canadian Families

Canadian families throw away an average of $1,300 worth of food every year. That's not just money — it's groceries you paid for, meals that never happened, and food ending up in landfill.

The good news? Most food waste is preventable with a few simple habits.

## 1. Know what you have before you shop

The number one cause of food waste is buying duplicates. You grab another bag of rice not realizing you already have two at home. A quick pantry check before every grocery run takes two minutes and saves real money.

## 2. Plan your meals for the week

Meal planning doesn't have to be complicated. Even a rough idea of what you'll cook Monday to Friday means you buy exactly what you need — nothing extra sitting in the fridge going bad.

## 3. Use the "first in, first out" rule

When you unpack groceries, move older items to the front of the fridge and cupboards. New items go to the back. You'll naturally reach for things that need to be used first.

## 4. Learn what expiry dates actually mean

"Best before" does not mean "unsafe after." Best before dates are about quality, not safety. Yogurt, cheese, and many packaged goods are often fine days after the date on the label. Use your senses — if it smells and looks fine, it usually is.

## 5. Store food properly

Half the battle is storage. Herbs last weeks longer in a glass of water in the fridge. Berries stay fresh longer if you wash them with a vinegar rinse before storing. Bread lasts longer in the freezer than on the counter.

## 6. Embrace the "use it up" meal

Pick one night a week — Sunday works well — where dinner is made entirely from whatever needs to be used. Fried rice, soups, stir fries, and frittatas are perfect for this. It's creative, cheap, and nothing goes to waste.

## 7. Freeze before it goes bad

If you know you won't use something in time, freeze it. Bread, meat, cooked grains, bananas, and most leftovers freeze well. Label everything with the date so you actually use it later.

## 8. Buy imperfect produce

Ugly fruits and vegetables taste exactly the same as the perfect-looking ones. Many Canadian grocery stores now sell imperfect produce at a discount. It's the same food, less waste, lower price.

## 9. Track your waste for one week

It sounds tedious but it is eye-opening. Keep a notepad on the fridge and write down everything you throw away for one week. You will quickly spot patterns — maybe it's always the spinach, always the leftovers from Tuesday. Fix the pattern, not the symptom.

## 10. Use a pantry tracking app

Knowing exactly what you have, what's expiring soon, and what to cook with it makes a real difference. Apps like Nooka track your pantry automatically, alert you before things expire, and suggest recipes based on what you already have — so nothing gets forgotten at the back of the fridge.

---

Small changes add up. Even cutting your food waste by half saves the average Canadian family over $600 a year. Start with one or two tips from this list and build from there.

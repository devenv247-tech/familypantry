# How to Eat Healthy on a Budget in Canada

Grocery prices in Canada have gone up significantly over the past few years. For families trying to eat well without overspending, it can feel like a constant juggling act.

The good news is that eating healthy doesn't have to mean spending more. Here's what actually works.

## Buy protein in bulk and freeze it

Meat is one of the biggest grocery expenses for most families. Buying larger packages — whole chickens, bulk ground beef, larger cuts — is almost always cheaper per kilogram than buying small portions. Portion it out at home and freeze what you won't use in the next two days.

## Lean on legumes

Lentils, chickpeas, black beans, and kidney beans are some of the most nutritious foods you can eat — and some of the cheapest. A bag of dried lentils costs under $3 and makes enough for multiple meals. They're high in protein, high in fibre, and incredibly versatile.

## Frozen vegetables are just as nutritious

Fresh vegetables are great but frozen vegetables are picked and frozen at peak ripeness, which means their nutritional value is often comparable to fresh — sometimes better if the fresh produce has been sitting in transit for days. Frozen spinach, peas, broccoli, and mixed vegetables are pantry staples that stretch any meal.

## Plan around what's on sale

Most grocery stores release their weekly flyers on Wednesday or Thursday. A quick look at what's on sale before you plan your meals for the week means you're building your menu around deals rather than paying full price for everything.

## Cook once, eat twice

Doubling a recipe takes almost no extra effort and gives you lunch or dinner for the next day. Soups, stews, curries, and casseroles all reheat well. Cooking from scratch in larger batches is almost always cheaper than buying ready-made or ordering takeout.

## Reduce food waste

The average Canadian family wastes over $1,300 in food every year. That's money you already spent, just not eaten. Tracking your pantry, using things before they expire, and planning meals around what you already have cuts waste and stretches your grocery budget further.

## Shop the perimeter, then the sales in the middle

The outer edges of most grocery stores — produce, meat, dairy — are where the whole foods live. The middle aisles have more processed options. Start on the perimeter for your staples, then dip into the middle aisles for pantry basics on sale.

## Use an app to track spending

Knowing where your grocery money actually goes is the first step to spending less. Tracking your purchases over a few weeks usually reveals patterns — maybe you're spending more than you realized on snacks, or buying produce that consistently goes to waste.
